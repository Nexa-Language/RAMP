With 22 model configurations now evaluated across four agent backends, the RAMP leaderboard tells a story that no static benchmark can capture. Today we're doing a deep dive into what the numbers actually mean — and why the resurrection mechanism is the most important innovation in agent evaluation this year.

🔑 Key Observation

Only 2 out of 22 configurations achieve zero-shot pipeline success (no resurrection needed). The other 20 all require at least one resurrection — meaning they fail at some point in the serial chain and need golden artifacts injected to continue. This is the fundamental insight of RAMP: serial dependency is hard, even for frontier models.

The Resurrection Effect: 17/19 Models Gain >40 Points

This is the most important number in the entire leaderboard. When we compare pipeline scores with resurrection vs. without resurrection:

  • 17 out of 19 configurations gain >40 pipeline score points
  • Median gain: 73.65 points
  • Mean gain: 71.55 points
  • Only GPT-5.5/Codex (zero-shot success) shows 0 gain

What does this mean? Without resurrection, most agents can only be evaluated at 1-2 tasks before cascading failure blocks everything downstream. The resurrection mechanism doesn't "help" agents — it reveals what they're actually capable of when given correct prerequisites.

Backend Matters: Kimi CLI vs. OpenHands

One of the most striking patterns is the backend effect. The same model (mimo-v2.5-pro) scores dramatically differently depending on the agent framework:

This 31-point gap is not about model capability — it's about scaffold design. Kimi CLI provides a different action space, context management strategy, and interaction loop than OpenHands. This confirms that agent scaffold is an independent variable that must be controlled and reported in benchmark evaluations.

The Process Metrics Paradox

We ran a regression analysis to see if process metrics (build count, elapsed time) could predict pipeline scores. The result: R² = 0.007. Process cost explains essentially zero percent of outcome variance.

This is not a failure — it's a finding. It means:

  • Spending more time or doing more builds does not lead to better scores
  • Some agents achieve high scores with minimal effort (efficient solvers)
  • Others exhaust their budget without progress (process collapse)
  • Process metrics capture qualitatively distinct information from outcome metrics
💡 Deployment Insight

If you're deploying AI coding agents in production, you need to monitor both outcome and process. An agent that achieves 95% accuracy but takes 8x longer than another agent with 90% accuracy may not be the right choice for your use case.
